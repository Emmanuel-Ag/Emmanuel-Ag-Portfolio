# 🎯 Lingr Network — Buyer Acquisition Framework

> The repeatable process we use to find premium buyers for every client.

---

## Overview

Most businesses struggle not because their product is bad — but because they're selling to the wrong people at the wrong price. This framework is how Lingr Network solves that.

It has **4 phases** and applies across industries: agribusiness, manufacturing, FMCG, services, and more.

---

## Phase 1 — Product & Market Assessment

Before we approach a single buyer, we understand what we're selling and who it's truly worth something to.

### What we document:
- [ ] What is the product? (physical goods, service, perishable?)
- [ ] What is the current selling price vs market ceiling?
- [ ] What is the volume available per week/month?
- [ ] What are the quality differentiators? (e.g. greenhouse-grown, organic, consistent sizing)
- [ ] What are the risk factors? (perishability, seasonality, supply gaps)

### Output:
A one-page **Product Value Summary** that frames the product in the language of premium buyers.

---

## Phase 2 — Buyer Targeting

Not all buyers are equal. We segment potential buyers by **willingness to pay** and **reliability**.

### Buyer Tiers

| Tier | Type | Price Tolerance | Reliability | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | End-use premium | Highest | High | Restaurants, hotels, caterers |
| Tier 2 | Distribution premium | High | Medium-High | Quality resellers, wholesalers |
| Tier 3 | Volume buyers | Medium | Medium | Supermarkets, market traders |
| Tier 4 | Spot buyers | Lowest | Low | Ad-hoc walk-ins |

**Rule:** Always lead with Tier 1 & 2 before falling back to Tier 3 or 4.

### Questions to identify the right buyers:
1. Who uses this product regularly and can't afford it to run out?
2. Who is currently buying a lower-quality version from a competitor?
3. Who pays a premium in adjacent categories? (e.g. a restaurant that buys premium tomatoes will buy premium cucumbers)
4. Who has a purchasing cycle that matches the client's supply cycle?

---

## Phase 3 — Outreach & Negotiation

### The Lingr Outreach Approach

We do not cold-pitch with generic offers. We lead with **value and specificity**.

**Opening Message Structure:**
```
[Greeting + credibility hook]
[Specific product offer — volume, quality, frequency]
[Why it fits THEIR business specifically]
[Proposed price + terms]
[Call to action — sample or meeting]
```

**Example (Cucumber Farm → Restaurant):**
> "Good afternoon — we supply greenhouse-grown cucumbers at consistent sizing and quality, available twice weekly. Given the volume your kitchen runs through, we can guarantee supply at ₦1,000/kg with no shortfalls. Would you be open to a trial delivery this week?"

### Negotiation Principles:
- **Anchor high.** Start at ceiling price — you can only go down.
- **Bundle reliability.** Premium buyers pay more for guaranteed supply, not just quality.
- **Lock in frequency.** A buyer who commits to twice weekly is worth 8x a once-a-month buyer.
- **Avoid spot pricing.** Every deal should have a recurring structure.

---

## Phase 4 — Retention & Expansion

Closing the first deal is just the start. Revenue compounds when buyers stay and grow.

### Retention Checklist:
- [ ] Deliver on time, every time — reliability is the product as much as the goods
- [ ] Communicate proactively about supply changes (seasonal gaps, harvest volumes)
- [ ] Check in monthly — buyer needs evolve
- [ ] Track buying frequency — any drop-off is an early warning sign

### Expansion Plays:
- Introduce the buyer to a second product when available (e.g. bell peppers after cucumbers)
- Ask for referrals — a satisfied restaurant buyer knows five other restaurant owners
- Renegotiate upward when supply improves or demand increases
